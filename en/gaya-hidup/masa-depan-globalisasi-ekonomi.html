Globalization on the Crossroad: Towards Shared Prosperity or Isolation?

Apa Itu Globalisasi dan Kenapa Dulunya Sangat Dipuja? (WHAT)

**Globalization** is simply the integration of cross-border economic activities. It includes the free flow of goods, services, capital, and people across the planet. The theory goes: if each country focuses on producing what they are good at (comparative advantage), then the total production of the world will increase, prices will be lower, and everyone will be better off.

During the era of the golden age (around 1990-2010), global trade grew incredibly fast. Millions of people, particularly in China and East Asia, escaped extreme poverty thanks to access to the global market. In the past, nearly all economists agreed that free trade was "blessed" because of its unmatched efficiency.

Who Are the Main Players and Who Are They Protesting Against? (WHO)

Initially, wealthy countries (G7, such as the United States and the United Kingdom) were both the main policemen and supporters of this system. International bodies like **IMF, World Bank, and WTO** played a crucial role in ensuring that the rules of the game were followed smoothly.

But not everyone was happy. There were groups of skeptics and anti-capitalist demonstrators who felt that globalization only benefited large corporations, at the expense of underpaid workers in developing countries. They pointed to phenomena like *sweatshops* (low-wage factories) as evidence. While in reality, foreign companies often pay higher wages than local businesses, negative sentiment about this remains strong.

Where Is Globalization Most Sensible and Where Is It Starting to Crumble? (WHERE)

Globalization is most evident in port and industrial cities like **Hangzhou in China** or the industrial areas in **Mexico**. On the other hand, older manufacturing cities in America like **Galax, Virginia**, feel devastated as local factories shut down due to being outcompeted by cheap imports from abroad.

Now, this "rift" is most visible at the **WTO in Geneva**. America has repeatedly blocked appointments for trade dispute court judges, making the organization resemble a "barnacle without a beak." The rules of globalization are starting to be replaced by regional blocs.

When Will These Changes Happen? (WHEN)

"Globalization used to move at the speed of light, but now crawls like a snail—this phenomenon is called **Slowglobalization**."

In the last 12 years, there have been three "death blows" to globalization:

  • **2008 Financial Crisis:** Caused many banks to pull foreign capital.
  • **US-China Trade War (2018):** Trump-era tariffs and protectionism started.
  • **2020 COVID-19 Pandemic:** Lockdowns cut global supply chains completely, making countries realize they were overly reliant on foreign sources.

Why Have We Moved to Homeland Economics? (WHY)

Now, a new trend is called **Homeland Economics** or "National Economics." Essentially, governments are no longer just thinking about "who is the cheapest," but rather "who is the safest."

Why? Because the world is getting less stable. Russia's invasion of Ukraine caused energy and wheat prices to soar, alerting many countries to geopolitical dependency being a huge danger. Additionally, there is concern that AI will make many people lose their jobs, leading governments to feel the need to "step in" to protect domestic industries through generous subsidies.

HOW

Instead of reducing trade barriers, countries are now competing to provide subsidies. The US has the *CHIPS Act* for the semiconductor industry, China has *Made in China 2025*, and India has production incentive schemes. This represents government intervention, which was once considered taboo in free-market economics.

The problem is, this approach raises production costs. In the past, products were produced where they were most efficient, but now products are produced "at home" even if it's more expensive. Long-term implications? World economic growth could slow down, and poor people in developing countries will find it even harder to keep up.

Conclusion: A Tighter and Costlier World 💸

Globalization is not dead, but it has become more "sectored." We are no longer living in a global village where everything feels the same, but in a divided world split into interest blocs. For us consumers, this means goods may not be as cheap as before. For businesses, this means supply chains must be shorter and more resilient.

Moral of the message: Openness (keterbukaan) is the key to prosperity, but national security has now become the top priority. We must be ready to adapt to a more "self-reliant" but possibly more expensive world. Keep up the good work and don't forget to keep learning!
